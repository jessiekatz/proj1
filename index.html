Did you think boba tea would become this popular?

Kai: "I didn't really think about what [boba's] future landscape was gonna look like...

Looking back though, it's a completely different story, and hindsight definitely helps. I did not expect boba to become what it is today.

You can see it in Jack in the Box, like, why the f**k is that there?"

Kai Chen has seen the growth of boba tea in the United States ever since it came to California in the 90s. Boba has been "nearly fully integrated into American culture." He sees advertisements for boba milkshakes in fast food chains and boba ice cream in Costco. While this has happened, he believes boba's value has been "cheapened."

There are 0 boba chains in the United States

Western fast food and food and beverage companies like Starbucks, Dunkin' Donuts and Jack in the Box have began to adopt the drink into their menus.

Brands have also popped up selling their version of boba.

One instance is Bobba, an Canadian company that appeared on Dragons' Den, a Canadian "Shark Tank". Guest judge Simu Liu called out the founders of Bobba on their pitch, which referred to boba tea as a "trendy sugary drink that you queue up for and you're never quite sure about its content."

“I know very well whats in it... There's an issue of taking something that's very distinctly Asian in its identity and 'making it better,' which I have an issue with” - Liu

There is a fine line between appreciation and appropriation when selling culture-specific items. In ignoring the cultural origins of the product, it overshadows its cultural significance.

Because beneath the attractive packaging...

...there's more substance to the sugar

Boba holds a much deeper significance to the Asian diaspora.

Boba shops have helped young Asian Americans find community in spaces that felt distant to them. For many, it is even an integral part of their identity.

YingYing Zhao wrote of her experience with boba in an article for the Yale Herald,

"I didn't have an American name, and I didn't have the same experiences as my mostly white peers.

but in high school, bubble tea was a connecting point... It was comforting and reassuring. It was a sense of belonging to a broader, Asian diasporic community."

I spoke to Kitty Vu, a Vietnamese-American, to learn more about her experience with boba growing up

Kitty:

"It seemed like everyone was getting boba in middle school... All your friends would be getting it after school. We would all go to Quickly and it felt really cool to do.

[Boba] seemed like something for Asians to connect and bond over. Especially growing up, I would be scared to eat home cooked foods at school.

It's definitely mainstream now, there's so many shops, so many chains. You don't see just Asians in boba shops anymore.

I'm glad that it's good food for everyone to enjoy, but I don't want to forget that Asian culture was not this widely accepted."

Vu shared that she felt some pushback against the growth of boba after her experience eating Vietnamese food at school.

"But what can I do, I'm glad that it's being accepted...

it does pave a pathway for other cultures... I see more Vietnamese Che shops so I'm glad that boba paved this way for other drinks to be shared."

Kai explained how he feels conflicted on its commercialization and how this brings changes to culture-specific foods like boba to fit a new audience.

"A part of me kind of dies when they change up a recipe or changing it for this other market. If Americans want to appropriate and bastardize it, I have mixed feelings on that...

On the other hand, Asian Americans have expressed their concerns of boba's significance in the community being diluted from this commercialization. Ying feels that it is losing its place as an "icon of the Asian American community."

"Boba only serves to make the largely diverse Asian diaspora more palatable to a 'Western' audience, and corporations capitalize on the internal perpetuation of a vapid boba culture."

...but looking back,

I'm happy that Americans have picked up on this stuff. I think it matters.

I think it's what makes America great, and I hate saying that phrase. It's the fact that the world comes to us. Boba came to us. We get the benefits of all this immigration" - Kai

Bubble tea is more than just a trend - its cultural roots helped Asian Americans build community in places that felt unwelcome or unfamiliar. Its appeal has opened doors for the acceptance of other Asian cultures. This commercialization has simultaneously brought on conflicting sentiments on boba's status in the Asian American community. But one thing is for sure -

Bubble tea isn't leaving the mainstream anytime soon.

With its growing popularity, western fast food and food and beverage giants have began to adopt the drink into their menus.

Kai believes that this has 'cheapened' boba's traditional and cultural past.

To many young Asian Americans, this adoption overshadows its significance within the community.

Because beneath the attractive packaging...

...there's more substance to the sugar

Boba holds a much deeper significance to the Asian diaspora.

Boba shops have helped young Asian Americans find community in spaces that felt distant to them. For many, it is even an integral part of their identity.

YingYing Zhao wrote of her experience with boba in an article for the Yale Herald,

"I didn't have an American name, and I didn't have the same experiences as my mostly white peers.

but in high school, bubble tea was a connecting point... It was comforting and reassuring. It was a sense of belonging to a broader, Asian diasporic community."

Kitty Vu views boba with similar sentiments. This is her experience with boba:

Kitty:

"It seemed like everyone was getting boba in middle school... All your friends would be getting it after school. We would all go to Quickly and it felt really cool to do.

[Boba] seemed like something for Asians to connect and bond over. Especially growing up, I would be scared to eat home cooked foods at school.

It's definitely mainstream now, there's so many shops, so many chains. You don't see just Asians in boba shops anymore.

I'm glad that it's good food for everyone to enjoy, but I don't want to forget that Asian culture was not this widely accepted."

Kitty shared that she felt some pushback towards the growth of boba after her experience eating Vietnamese food at school.

"But what can I do, I'm glad that it's being accepted...

it does pave a pathway for other cultures... I see more Vietnamese Che shops so I'm glad that boba paved this way for other drinks to be shared."

On the other hand, Ying feels that it is losing its place as an "icon of the Asian American community."

"Boba only serves to make the largely diverse Asian diaspora more palatable to a 'Western' audience, and corporations capitalize on the internal perpetuation of a vapid boba culture."

Kai explained how he feels conflicted on its commercialization and how this brings changes to culture-specific foods like boba to fit a new audience.

"A part of me kind of dies when they change up a recipe or changing it for this other market. If Americans want to appropriate and bastardize it, I have mixed feelings on that...

...but looking back, I'm happy that Americans have picked up on this stuff. I think it matters.

I think it's what makes America great, and I hate saying that phrase. It's the fact that the world comes to us. Boba came to us. We get the benefits of all this immigration" - Kai

Bubble tea is more than just a trend

Its cultural roots helped Asian Americans build community in places that felt unwelcome or unfamiliar.

This commercialization has brought on conflicting sentiments on boba's status in the Asian American community

while also opening doors for other Asian cultures in the West.

But one thing is for sure -

Bubble tea isn't leaving the mainstream anytime soon.
